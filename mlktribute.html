The Man

Martin Luther King Jr. was born on January 15, 1929 to his father, the Sr., and his mother Alberta. Both of his parents were college-educated, with his father serving as the pastor of a prestigious Atlanta church. King was the second of three children, with an older sister Christine and a younger brother Alfred Daniel (A.D.)

Originally born Michael Luther King Jr., King was five years old when his father changed both his own name, as well as his son’s, from “Michael Luther” to “Martin Luther”. His father made the change after travelling in Europe, where he was inspired by the legacy of the Reformation leader Martin Luther.

King experienced bigotry and prejudice from a young age, once remarking that at age six he was not able to play with a friend of his any longer because his friend’s parents would not allow their white son to play with a black child.

At fifteen years old he was admitted to Morehouse College, where he spent three years before beginning his bachelor of divinity at Crozer Theological Seminary, graduating in 1951 at the age of 22. He would eventually go on to serve as co-pastor at the Ebeneezer Baptist Church in Atlanta, alongside his father, from 1960 up until his death.

From Crozer he went on to Boston University, where he met his wife Coretta Scott as the result of a blind date, marrying her in 1953. Together they would have four children: Yolanda, Martin Luther III, Dexter Scott, and Bernice.

He and his wife were approaching their 15-year wedding anniversary when Dr. King was assassinated on April 4, 1968.

The Leader

King’s first significant leadership role came as the head of the Montgomery Improvement Association, leading a boycott of the transit system in response to the actions of Rosa Parks in 1955. Shortly afterwards, King was elected president of the Southern Christian Leadership Conference, an organization formed to provide new leadership for the burgeoning civil rights movement.

In the eleven-year period between 1957 and 1968, King traveled over six million miles and spoke over twenty-five hundred times, appearing wherever there was injustice, protest, and action. His public image put his safety at risk on multiple fronts, with the first attempt on his life taking place in 1958 when he was stabbed in the chest by a woman with severe mental illness.

King delivered one of the most famous speeches in history – the “I Have a Dream” speech – as one of the leaders of the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom on August 28, 1963.

King’s company was sought out by sitting Presidents and world leaders during a time when he was arrested on over two dozen occasions and assaulted by officials on at least four occasions. He was awarded five honorary degrees, was named Man of the Year by Time magazine in 1963, and became not only the symbolic leader of American blacks but also a world figure. At the age of thirty-five, King was the youngest man to ever be awarded the Nobel Peace Prize.

In 1983, President Reagan signed Martin Luther King Jr. Day into law as a federal holiday. King is only the second American to have a federal holiday named in his honor, the other being George Washington.
